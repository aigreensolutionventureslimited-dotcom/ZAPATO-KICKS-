EXPLORING ALTERNATIVE CAREER OPPORTUNITIES FOR NAUTICAL SCIENCE GRADUATES BEYOND ONBOARD SEAFARING

1) Name of Journal & Author Maritime Professional Review | Anointed Okowhoake
2) Date of Publication February 19, 2026
3) Main Objectives Identify barriers to sea employment and map shore-based maritime pathways.
4) Research Methods Qualitative Descriptive Analysis & Professional Competency Mapping.

CHAPTER ONE: INTRODUCTION

1.1 Background of the Study

Nautical Science is a professional maritime discipline focused on navigation, ship handling, seamanship, maritime law, cargo operations, and safety management. Traditionally, graduates of Nautical Science are trained to pursue careers as deck officers onboard merchant vessels, progressing from cadet to captain.

However, due to factors such as limited sea berths, economic downturns, automation, and intense global competition, many graduates are unable to secure onboard employment. This situation creates uncertainty and career stagnation among cadets.

1.2 Statement of the Problem

Many Nautical Science cadets struggle to obtain sea-time opportunities after graduation. Since sea-time is mandatory for certification, failure to secure onboard employment can permanently halt professional advancement. There is a critical lack of awareness regarding alternative maritime and shore-based careers, resulting in underemployment.

1.3 Aim of the Study

To examine alternative career opportunities available to Nautical Science graduates who are unable to secure onboard employment.

1.4 Objectives of the Study

  • To identify challenges preventing cadets from obtaining sea employment.
  • To examine shore-based maritime career opportunities.
  • To analyze transferable skills acquired in Nautical Science training.
  • To recommend career diversification strategies.

CHAPTER TWO: LITERATURE REVIEW

2.1 Overview of Nautical Science Training

Nautical Science provides comprehensive knowledge in navigation, ship stability, meteorology, maritime law, and safety response. These skills are highly transferable beyond shipboard operations.

2.2 Challenges in Securing Sea Employment

Studies show a global oversupply of junior officers in certain regions. Shipping companies often prefer experienced cadets, and the lack of strong national shipping fleets in developing nations worsens these opportunities.

CHAPTER THREE: ALTERNATIVE CAREER PATHWAYS

3.1 Port and Terminal Operations

  • Port operations officer, Terminal planner, and Berth coordinator.

3.2 Maritime Safety and Compliance

  • Safety officer (ISM compliance), Port State Control assistant, and Marine surveyor.

3.3 Shipping and Logistics Industry

  • Shipping operations executive, Chartering assistant, and Supply chain coordinator.

3.4 Maritime Administration & Government

  • Maritime administrations, Port authorities, and Customs services.

CHAPTER FOUR: SKILLS ANALYSIS

Nautical cadets possess analytical thinking, situational awareness, technical documentation ability, and discipline. These skills are highly employable in non-maritime sectors like Aviation operations, Emergency management, and Corporate risk management.

5) SUMMARY

This research provides a comprehensive roadmap for Nautical Science graduates. It establishes that while seafaring is the primary goal, the "Blue Economy" offers an expansive array of shore-based roles that require the exact technical and legal knowledge taught in the Nautical Science curriculum at FCFMT. It concludes that a Nautical Science degree is a versatile management asset, not just a license to sail.

6) THE RESEARCH GAP

Current maritime literature focuses almost exclusively on the technical efficiency of vessels and onboard crew retention. There is a significant gap in structured academic guidance for the "Shore-side Transition" of deck officers. This study fills that gap by providing a strategic framework for career diversification that is currently missing from standard maritime vocational counseling.

CHAPTER FIVE: RECOMMENDATIONS

  • Maritime institutions should provide career counseling beyond sea service.
  • Curriculum should include maritime business and logistics modules.
  • Governments should strengthen local shipping fleets.
  • Cadets should acquire additional certifications in project management or HSE.
